⚽ FIFA World Cup 2026: Team & Player Value Research Dashboard

What started as a pandas cleanup exercise on ~54,600 match rows turned into a full scouting report: a homegrown Player Value Score, a look at what actually moves a transfer price, and a regression model that puts a euro figure on where every player might land by 2027. 💡 Key Insights

What I Learned

01

Don't trust a dataset's built-in totals. The columns that were supposed to already have each player's season totals didn't match what you get from adding up the raw match data yourself — so every total in this project was rebuilt from scratch instead of taking the shortcut.

02

Compare players within their own position, not everyone at once. A defender and a striker do very different jobs, so scoring them on one universal scale isn't fair. Ranking players only against others in the same position gave results that actually made sense.

03

Flashy stats drive price more than steady ones. Goals, assists, and overall rating line up with market value far more than defensive stats do — even though solid defending matters just as much to winning.

04

The model explains part of the picture, not the whole thing. It only accounts for around half of what actually drives a player's value, so it's good for spotting players worth a second look, but not precise enough to set an exact price.

05

The same method spots both bargains and red flags. Comparing a player's projected future value to their price today doesn't just surface undervalued young players — it also flags players who might be overpriced for what they actually produce.

🧮 Custom Metric

Building a Player Value Score From Scratch

Instead of trusting the dataset's own rating column, every player is scored on five percentile-ranked categories — attacking, creativity, defensive work, physical output, and reliability — using per-90-minute rates so a sub who only played 800 minutes isn't punished next to a player who logged 1,700. Weights shift by position (a center-back's defensive percentile matters far more than a striker's), and goalkeepers get their own shot-stopping-plus-distribution formula entirely. As a gut check, the score still correlates strongly with the dataset's built-in rating — it isn't measuring noise.

🔍 Reality Check

What Actually Moves a Transfer Price

Market value is badly skewed — a handful of superstar valuations drag the whole scale — so it's log-transformed before comparing it against every per-90 stat on offer. Of the 24 candidate metrics tested, these 14 correlate most strongly (in either direction) with value; the rest sat close enough to zero to not be worth cluttering the chart with. Green climbs with value, red falls with it. The headline: end product (goals, assists, xG/xA, overall rating) tracks value far more tightly than the unglamorous defensive work that actually wins matches.

🤖 Machine Learning

Putting a Number on 2027

A plain Linear Regression and a Random Forest were both trained on the same performance stats, age, and position, then judged only on data neither model had seen during training. The twist: the extra complexity of a forest of decision trees barely moves the needle here — a useful result in itself, since it means the simpler, more explainable model isn't leaving much on the table.

💰 Scouting Report

Who's Underpriced Right Now

Every player's model-projected 2027 value (run through a transparent age-curve adjustment, not a black box) is compared against what they're worth today, as a percentage gap rather than a flat euro amount — a €5M swing means a completely different thing for a €2M academy prospect than for an €80M superstar. It cuts both ways, too: the second chart flips the list upside down to show who the model thinks is priced above their output.
